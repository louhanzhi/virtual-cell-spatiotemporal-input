18 Abstract
19 Gene regulatory networks (GRNs) dynamically regulate gene activation and repression, 20 driving cellular differentiation. Despite advancements in GRN inference, challenges 21 remain in capturing differentiation dynamics and causal inference. To address these 22 limitations, we developed TRIGON, a Transformer-based model that infers dynamic 23 GRN through learning temporal causality among genes. TRIGON achieved state-of24 the-art performance, improving accuracy by 204% over the latest methods across four 25 developmental datasets. When applied to well-established paradigms, including mouse 26 embryonic stem cell and hematopoietic stem cell differentiation, TRIGON identified 27 key transcription factors (TFs) that were not detectable by differential expression 28 analysis and revealed potential TFs associated with primitive endoderm differentiation. 29 Furthermore, TRIGON constructed dynamic GRN across varying time resolutions, 30 enabling the analysis of GRN dynamics from diverse time scales. Through in silico 31 perturbation, TRIGON accurately recapitulated cell fate changes following Gata1 and 32 Spi1 knockout and accurately predicted gene expression changes.

33 Introduction
34 Gene regulatory networks (GRNs) consist of transcription factors (TFs) that bind to 35 DNA regulatory elements, either promoting or inhibiting the expression of their target 36 genes (TGs) during cellular differentiation1. These networks ensure that specific genes 37 are activated or repressed at the right time and location, determining the cell fate. Over 38 the decades, numerous methods have been proposed to infer cell lineage GRN from 39 bulk transcriptomic data2-4. However, these bulk-based approaches require a sufficient 40 number of transcriptomic samples to satisfy statistical assumptions, severely limiting 41 their practicality. The advent of single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq)5 enables 42 high-throughput profiling of thousands of cells simultaneously, spurring the 43 development of a variety of GRN inference methods. For instance, WGCNA6 and 44 GENIE37 inferred GRN by capturing the co-variation between TFs and TGs, 45 CellOracle8 leveraged bagging ridge regression to infer cell-type specific GRN using 46 scRNA-seq and chromatin accessibility data, and CEFCON9 utilized graph neural 47 network alongside prior knowledge to model cell lineage GRN. 48 49 Despite significant advancements, several critical challenges in GRN inference remain 50 unresolved. First, common approaches for constructing cell lineage GRN involve 51 clustering cells and treat each cluster as a distinct cell type8, 10, 11, followed by inferring 52 GRN independently for each cluster (Figure 1A). However, these clustering-based 53 methods inherently discretize the continuous developmental cells into separate bins, 54 which undermine the ability to capture the cellular differentiation dynamics. Moreover, 55 GRNs inferred by these methods exhibit time resolution limited to the cluster level, 56 meaning that the regulatory strength of TF-TG edges is only distinguished between 57 clusters. They assume that cells within the same cluster share an identical GRN, but 58 actually these cells exhibit developmental heterogeneity. Second, most existing 59 methods focus on inferring static GRNs8, 9, 12, 13, where the regulatory strength between 60 TFs and TGs is assumed to remain constant over time. While Dictys14 represents a 61 notable effort to model dynamic GRN, it imposes numerous constraints on the moving 62 window used for constructing dynamic network, which result in a fixed time resolution, 63 limiting the ability to analyze GRN dynamics across varying time scales. Third, current 64 cell lineage GRN inference methods often obtain suboptimal accuracy, with 65 performance only slightly surpassing that of random predictions15. 66 67 To address the above challenges, we proposed TRIGON (Transformer-based method 68 for inference of dynamic gene regulatory network), which focused on inferring cell 69 lineage GRN. TRIGON extended the concept of granger causality to the context of gene 70 regulation, enabling the inference of dynamic GRN and deciphering GRN dynamics 71 across different time resolutions. We evaluated TRIGON against other latest methods 72 across four developmental datasets and observed 204% improvement in inference 73 accuracy. To demonstrate its applicability, we applied TRIGON to multiple well74 characterized biological systems, including the differentiation processes of mouse 75 embryonic stem cell (mESC) and hematopoietic stem cell (mHSC). 84 Overview of TRIGON:
85 Granger causality16 is a statistical concept used to determine whether one time series 86 can predict another. If variations in a time series X over a specific period are 87 consistently followed by corresponding variations in a time series Y after a certain time 88 lag, X is said to Granger-cause Y (Figure 1B, left). We posited that the temporal 89 causality emphasized in granger causality aligns with the fundamental principles of 90 GRNs. TFs regulate TGs by binding to their regulatory elements, thereby modulating 91 transcription rates. Consequently, for a given TG, its expression after time point t is 92 influenced by the expression of its TFs at preceding time points (Figure 1B, right). It 93 is GRN that establishes the causal relationships between the gene expression of TFs 94 and their TGs. Therefore, we formulated an inverse problem: can the temporal 95 causality between the gene expression of TFs and their TGs be learned and then 96 infer the underlying regulatory network? To address this, we developed a 97 Transformer-based model, TRIGON, which incorporated two uniquely designed masks 98 to learn the temporal causality in gene regulation and ultimately inferred the dynamic 99 cell lineage GRN. 100 101 Here, we define two key terms: (1) watching window, representing the time window for 102 observed gene expression. 2) forecasting window, representing the time window for 103 gene expression that will be predicted. TRIGON took gene expression in watching 104 window and a base GRN as input and output the predicted gene expression in 105 forecasting window and a learned GRN coefficient matrix. The observed gene 106 expression will first pass through the Temporal aggregation module, which aggregated 107 the historical expression information for each gene (Figure 1C, Methods). At each 108 time point, genes will attend its historical expression from all preceding time points and 109 refine its value as a weighted sum of them. The aggregated gene expression data, 110 together with the base GRN, were then processed by the Regulatory filtering module, 111 which assigned a list of potential TFs to each TG, ensuring each TG's expression was 112 predicted solely based on the expression of its potential TFs. 113 114 TRIGON is constructed by Transformer encoders, leveraging the self-attention 115 mechanism17 to capture temporal causality among genes (Figure 1D, Methods). In the 116 Temporal aggregation module, an upper triangular mask is introduced to ensure that the 117 expression of a gene at a given time point can only attend to expression information 118 from preceding time points. In the Regulatory filtering module, the base GRN is utilized 119 as a binary mask, allowing each TG to attend only to TFs connected via edges present 120 in the base GRN. Finally, TRIGON predict expression of TG in forecasting window, 121 which is evaluated against observed ground truth using the Mean Squared Error (MSE) 122 loss. After training, we utilize the inference module to obtain GRN (Extended Figure 123 1A, Methods). Specifically, the GRN coefficient matrix is extracted from the 124 Regulatory filtering module, where each value represents the regulatory strength

129 TRIGON shows state-of-art performance in extensive benchmarks
130 We first evaluated the accuracy of cell lineage GRNs inferred by TRIGON on mESC 131 and mHSC datasets, both are provided by BEELINE15, a benchmarking study for GRN 132 inference. The mHSC dataset has three sub-lineage datasets: mHSC-E (Erythroid 133 lineage), mHSC-GM (Granulocyte-Monocyte lineage) and mHSC-L (Lymphoid 134 lineage). For each lineage, we used available ChIP-seq data provided by BEELINE as 135 the ground truth GRN to benchmark the reconstructed GRN. Testing data for each 136 lineage was sampled based on watching windows. Each window generated a GRN 137 through the inference module, and GRNs from different windows were averaged to 138 construct the final cell lineage GRN (Figure 2A, Methods). 139 140 The accuracy of the inferred GRN can be evaluated using metrics employed in binary 141 classification problems (Figure 2B, Methods). We used the area under the receiver 142 operating characteristic curve (AUROC), area under the precision-recall curve 143 (AUPRC) as metrics and compared TRIGON against seven GRN inference methods: 144 (1) GENIE37 and GRNBoost218, which are widely used classical methods for GRN 145 inference. (2) CEFCON9 and NetREX19, which require prior network as input to infer 146 GRN. (3) CellOracle8, a multimodal machine learning method leveraging chromatin 147 accessibility data to provide prior knowledge for GRN inference. (4) Random, which 148 randomly selects k edges in all potential gene edges (k equals to the number of edges 149 in the ground truth GRN) and (5) Prior Random, which randomly selects k edges in 150 base GRN. 151 152 The receiver operating characteristic (ROC) curves of all methods (Figure 2D) 153 indicated that TRIGON achieved the highest AUROC on the mESC, mHSC-GM, and 154 mHSC-E datasets, surpassing the second-best methods (GENIE3, NetREX, and 155 CellOracle) by 27%, 33%, and 0.7%. We observed that previously proposed methods 156 showed no significant performance improvement compared to Random. For instance, 157 on the mESC dataset, NetREX (0.522), GRNBoost2 (0.537), and GENIE3 (0.545) 158 performed only marginally better than Random (0.506), indicating that the accuracy of 159 these methods in GRN inference is barely above random guessing. However, TRIGON 160 achieved an AUROC of 0.693, demonstrating the model's superior performance. When 161 analyzing the precision-recall (PR) curve (Figure 2E), we observed that all baseline 162 methods exhibited a significant decrease in performance, with most methods achieving 163 AUPRC below 0.2. In contrast, TRIGON achieved an average AUPRC of 0.752 across 164 four datasets, representing a 204% improvement over the latest method CellOracle 165 (0.247). To investigate the underlying reason, we examined the label distribution within 166 each dataset and found a pronounced class imbalance across all datasets, with positive 167 labels accounting for only 1.8%, 1.3%, 1.4%, and 2.3%, respectively (Figure 2C,

These findings indicated that the enhanced performance of 188 TRIGON was not attributable to the quality of the base GRN but rather to the intrinsic 189 strength of itself. 190
191 Identification of key TFs in mESC differentiation
192 Identifying key TFs during cellular differentiation is pivotal for elucidating the 193 regulatory mechanisms that govern cell fate. We applied TRIGON to the mESC dataset 194 (Figure 3A), which comprised scRNA-seq data collected at five distinct time points (0, 195 12, 24, 48, and 72 hours) as mouse embryonic stem cells progress toward primitive 196 endoderm (PrE). We utilized AUCell10 (Extended Figure 1B, Methods) to calculate 197 the activity score of each TF regulon in individual cells based on the inferred GRN. A 198 TF regulon is defined as a gene set comprising the TF itself and its TGs (Figure 3B). 199 The activity score reflects the proportion of genes within the TF regulon that are highly 200 expressed in a given cell, with a higher score indicating that the TF regulon is more 201 active in that cell. 306 In silico perturbation in mouse hematopoietic stem cells
307 The objective of constructing cell lineage GRN is to uncover the underlying 308 mechanisms of cellular differentiation, ultimately enabling the manipulation of cell fate. 309 Perturbation experiments serve as a critical approach for achieving this goal. We 310 employed a method inspired by CellOracle8 to evaluate whether the predicted GRN 311 could recapitulate the fate changes following the knockout of key TFs. The GRN 312 inferred by TRIGON was represented as a directed graph, with edges indicating 313 regulatory strengths from TFs to TGs. Perturbing a specific TF propagated the effects 314 to its TGs along these edges, where the extent of the effect on each TG is determined 315 by the edge weight (Figure 5A). To simulate gene knockout, the expression of the TF 316 was set to zero, resulting in a perturbed expression vector. This vector was subsequently 317 multiplied by the GRN coefficient matrix inferred by TRIGON, propagating the 318 perturbation effects to the TGs (Extended Figure 1C, Methods). By iteratively 319 propagating these effects, we computed the final expression difference vector for each 320 cell and derived the perturbation vector (Extended Figure 1D, Methods). The vector 321 indicates the direction of fate change for each cell following the perturbation (Figure 322 5A, Methods). 323 324 We applied TRIGON to perform in silico perturbation on the mHSC dataset, which has 325 several key cellular states: (1) hematopoietic stem cell (HSC), (2) multipotent 326 progenitor (MPP), (3) lymphoid multipotent progenitor (LMPP), (4) common myeloid 327 progenitor (CMP), (5) megakaryocyte-erythrocyte progenitor (MEP), and (6) 328 granulocyte-monocyte progenitor (GMP) (Figure 5B). We focused on two well329 characterized TFs, Gata1 and Spi1, to investigate cell fate changes following in silico 330 knockout. Previous studies have demonstrated that Gata1 promotes erythroid lineage 331 development in HSC and facilitates the differentiation of CMP into MEP35-37. In 332 contrast, Spi1 enhances LMPP differentiation and promotes the differentiation of CMP 333 into GMP while simultaneously inhibits their differentiation into MEP38 (Figure 5C). 334 335 We conducted in silico perturbation of Gata1 in the erythroid lineage (Figure 5D). Each 336 cell was colored based on its pseudotime, and the pseudotime gradient vector was 337 computed (Methods). 367 Discussion
368 Understanding the GRNs underlying cell lineage development is crucial for deciphering 369 the molecular mechanisms that govern cell fate decisions. GRN provides a framework 370 to map the intricate interactions among TFs and their target genes, enabling researchers 371 to explore how specific regulatory elements drive cellular identity and behavior during 372 lineage progression. Accurate reconstruction of cell lineage GRN is vital for advancing 373 our knowledge in developmental biology, regenerative medicine, and disease modeling. 374 375 Previous methods for GRN inference have predominantly concentrated on static 376 networks, which operate under the assumption that TF-TG regulatory strengths remain 377 constant over time. However, these approaches fail to account for the dynamic changes 378 in regulatory interactions that occur as cells transition through distinct states along their 379 differentiation process. Such limitations hinder a comprehensive understanding of the 380 temporal dynamics by which GRN orchestrates lineage commitment and differentiation 381 processes. 382 383 We observed the granger causality in gene regulatory processes, motivating us to infer 384 GRN by analyzing the temporal causality between TF and TG expression. To achieve 385 this, we employed the self-attention mechanism from Transformer to model temporal 386 correlation among genes, framing GRN inference as a multivariate time series 387 prediction problem. TRIGON designed two unique masks, a temporal mask aggregated 388 historical expression information for each gene, and a regulatory filter mask assigned 389 potential TFs to each TG. By predicting TG expression in the forecasting window based 390 on TF expression in the watching window, TRIGON learned the causal relationship 391 between the two time series and output a GRN coefficient matrix, which was 392 subsequently post-processed to construct the final GRN. 393 394 TRIGON demonstrated state-of-the-art performance across four developmental 395 datasets, achieving significant improvements in AUROC and AUPRC. Notably, 396 TRIGON achieved an average AUPRC of 0.752, representing a 204% improvement 397 over the latest CellOracle method. We applied TRIGON to analyze the differentiation 398 of mESC and identified three classes of key TFs active at distinct stages of 399 differentiation. Beyond recognizing well-known TFs such as Nanog, Sox2, and Pou5f1, 400 TRIGON also uncovered potential TFs associated with PrE differentiation and 401 identified some TFs that DEA could not distinguish. Additionally, we constructed 402 dynamic GRN for mESC differentiation, enabling the observation of GRN dynamics at 403 various time resolutions. During mHSC differentiation, TRIGON successfully 404 recapitulated cell fate changes following the knockouts of Gata1 and Spi1 and validated 405 TRIGON's ability to predict the gene expression changes after perturbation. 406 407 TRIGON offers a novel approach to dynamic GRN construction through time series 408 prediction. The flexible time resolution provided by TRIGON opens new avenues for

550 Influence of base GRN
551 TRIGON uses the base GRN as a mask in the Regulatory filtering module, allowing to 552 learn the regulatory relationships only between edges present in the base GRN. This 553 approach narrows the search space for gene pairs. To demonstrate that the reduction in 554 search space is not the main factor contributing to the model's performance 555 improvement, we evaluated the impact of the base GRN on model accuracy. 556 Specifically, we introduced a method called Prior Random during benchmarking to 557 verify the accuracy of randomly selecting the top k edges from the base GRN. We found 558 that the AUROC of this method was not significantly different from the Random 559 method. This suggests that the base GRN contains substantial noise, with many false 560 positive edges, leading to performance comparable to random guessing. To further 561 support this, we calculated the number of edges in the base GRN and their overlap with 562 the ground truth GRN. The statistics, provided in the Extended Table 1, show that 563 approximately 99.5% of the edges in the base GRN are false positives. Additionally, 564 the benchmarking methods we compared against, including CEFCON, NetREX, and 565 CellOracle, also employed the base GRN to reduce the search space. Our performance 566 demonstrates significant improvement over these methods across all datasets, 567 suggesting that the base GRN is not the key factor driving the performance 568 improvement of our model. 569

570 AUCeLL algorithm
571 To identify key TFs during cellular differentiation based on the inferred GRN, we 572 employed the AUCell algorithm to calculate the activity score for each TF. AUCell first 573 ranks gene expression in each cell from high to low and sets a threshold (defaulting to 574 the top 5%) to identify highly expressed genes in that cell. For each threshold (ranging 575 from 0 to 5%), the algorithm calculates the proportion of genes within the TF regulon 576 that are highly expressed in the given cell. By computing the area under the curve 577 (AUC), the algorithm determines the activity score of each TF in the cell. A higher 578 score indicates that the TF is more actively expressed in the given cell. Since the mESC 579 dataset includes five sequencing time points (0h, 12h, 24h, 48h, and 72h), we performed

896 Figure 1. GRN inference process and workflows of AUCell, in silico perturbation. A, After 897 training, TRIGON processes the testing data through its Regulatory filtering module to infer 898 the GRN coefficient matrix. Regulatory scores are calculated for each TF-TG edge based on 899 this matrix, and the top k edges are selected as the final inferred GRN. B, Threshold of AUCell 900 is progressively increased to determine how many genes in each TF regulon are highly 901 expressed in a given cell. The area under the resulting curve is computed as the TF's activity 902 score. C, The effects of gene perturbations are propagated according to the GRN coefficient 903 matrix. After each propagation step, the value of the perturbed gene is reset to its initial 904 perturbation value to ensure the perturbation's influence persists throughout the whole 905 propagation process. D, Once the final expression difference vector for a cell is obtained, the 906 transition probabilities are calculated by comparing the vector to the original expression 907 difference vectors of its neighboring cells. These probabilities are combined with the coordinate 908 difference vectors in the 2D space of the cells to compute a weighted sum, resulting in the 909 perturbation vector for the cell.
