Abstract

25

Live-cell imaging uniquely captures single-cell dynamics in space and time, but

26

robust analysis is limited by segmentation and tracking errors that accumulate

27

across frames. We present LivecellX, a deep-learning­based pipeline that inte-

28

grates instance-level segmentation error correction with trajectory refinement,

29

leveraging temporal context to recover accurate cell tracks. LivecellX also intro-

30

duces a benchmark dataset with detailed annotations of common error classes,

31

providing a resource for method development and evaluation. Beyond error cor-

32

rection, the framework incorporates modules for classifying biological processes,

33

reconstructing cell lineages, and analyzing dynamic behaviors. Users can interact

34

with the system programmatically or through a Napari-based graphical inter-

35

face, enabling flexible integration into diverse workflows. By coupling error-aware

36

correction with comprehensive lineage and dynamics analysis, LivecellX estab-

37

lishes an open, extensible platform that advances the accuracy and scalability of

38

live-cell imaging studies.

42 1 Introduction

43 Advances in single-cell techniques have transformed the characterization of cellu-

44 lar states-phenotype, transcriptomic, proteomic, epigenomic, and metabolic-and their

45 dynamics. These methods are broadly divided into sequencing- and imaging-based,

46 or alternatively into destructive snapshot approaches and nondestructive methods

47 that enable longitudinal cell tracking. Most sequencing- and multiplex staining-based

48 imaging techniques are destructive [1­8].

49 Unlike computational inference of cell-state transitions from snapshots, live-cell

50 imaging directly captures spatial and temporal dynamics [9­12], but its adoption

51 lags behind destructive high-throughput methods due to technical challenges. One

52 main challenge is cell segmentation, which must be highly accurate to enable reliable

53 tracking and quantification of features such as morphology and protein distribution

54 [13]. Deep learning, particularly convolutional neural networks and vision trans-

55 formers, has enabled large-scale segmentation [8, 14­19], and tools like CellPose

56 and µSAM represent current state-of-the-art. However, no method achieves per-

57 fect accuracy [20]; segmentation errors, especially under-segmentation(under-seg) and

58 over-segmentation(over-seg), remain common and propagate into tracking, where

59 performance depends heavily on segmentation quality (Fig.1a)[21­25].

60

Consider a live-cell imaging experiment that images 102­104 cells simultaneously

61 every 10 min for a duration of 16 hours, generating movies with  100 frames. With

62 a segmentation accuracy of 98%, the probability of tracking a trajectory without any

63 segmentation errors is merely approximately 0.98100  0.13(Fig. 1b). For a seven day

64 experiment, the accuracy further reduces to 0.981008  1 × 10-9. With a large long-

65 time imaging dataset, e.g., the above-mentioned seven-day experiment with a total of

66  105­107 cells, it is impractical to identify and correct a fraction of  103­105 mis-

67 segmented cells through visual inspection. In applications, label-free live-cell imaging

68 emerges as an appealing alternative to fluorescent imaging because of the reduced

69 photo-toxicity and no requirement of genetic engineering [11]. Due to the lack of

70 corresponding training data, segmentation accuracy is typically even lower than that

71 achieved with fluorescent images.

72 Even worse is that segmentation errors often intertwine with single cell trajectory

73 tracking. For instance, one segmentation error in time frame t together with movement

74 of cells may generate errors not only in that specific cell trajectory but also in tracking

75 errors in trajectories of neighboring cells (Fig. 1c).

76 Cell division also increases the complexity of extracting trajectories [26]. Identifi-

77 cation of cell division is the key step in lineage tracing that is crucial for analyzing

78 long-term live-cell imaging data [27]. But the scarcity of cell division poses challenges

79 for deep learning models that rely on large and balanced training datasets. For cell

80 dynamics analyses one needs to extract high-dimensional features and choose appro-

81 priate representations for cell states [10, 28, 29]. However, limitation in techniques of

82 microscopy and labeling imposes challenges on the widely used multiplex fluorescent

83 labeling for feature extraction in snap-shot imaging experiment. Furthermore, fluores-

84 cence labeling can induce significant photo-toxicity in cells during long-term live-cell

85 imaging [27].In recent years, label-free transmitted light imaging emerges as an alter-

86 native imaging modality, with significantly enhanced reliability and repeatability of

87 experiments, particularly for long-term live-cell imaging studies.

88 For label-free or low-plex fluorescent imaging, morphological and textural features

89 are widely used to represent cell states and analyze trajectory dynamics [7, 10, 28, 30­

90 33]. Hence, software package should include modules for label-free feature extraction

91 and dynamical systems theory-based trajectory analysis[10, 34, 35].

92 While many computational tools exist for snapshot imaging [7, 8], specialized pack-

93 ages for live-cell imaging remain scarce. Although several solutions support single-cell

94 tracking and trajectory analysis that tackle some but not all the associated challenges

95 simultaneously [26, 36­39]. For instance, most methods rely on stained nuclei [40]

96 and manually curated features, with only a few applying deep learning [26, 41]. The

97 growing scale of live-cell imaging further demands computational efficiency, memory

In this study, we present LivecellX, a comprehensive workflow for analyzing single- 100

cell trajectories from time-lapse imaging datasets. The contribution of LivecellX is 101

threefold. First, it provides an automated procedure to detect and correct under-seg 102

and over-seg errors as well as tracking errors by developing a deep-learning-based 103

method, Corrective Segmentation Network (CS-Net), and integrating temporal infor- 104

mation. Second, the LivecellX architecture is designed to transform the analysis task 105

from an image-matrix-oriented processing paradigm to a single-cell object-oriented 106

paradigm. Based on the LivecellX architecture, we integrated methods and designed 107

programming interfaces for tracking, extracting features, detecting biological process, 108

lineage reconstruction, and dynamics analyses. Finally, LivecellX is integrated with 109

Napari to allow interactive user-in-the-loop analyses of live-cell data trajectory objects 110

and cell objects.

2.1 Overview of the LivecellX architecture and workflow

113

LivecellX is a comprehensive framework for live-cell imaging data analyses (Fig. 1d). It 114

is designed to improve cell segmentation and tracking accuracy based on the additional 115

temporal information between consecutive frames in live-cell imaging, and simplify 116

trajectory dynamics extraction and complex cellular process analysis.

117

LivecellX takes cell segmentation and trajectory tracking results from other meth- 118

ods as input (Fig. 1e), saves them as a collection of single cell trajectories (Fig. 1f). The 119

segmentation and trajectory results are fed into and iterate between an inconsistency 120

identification module and the CS-Net (Fig. 1g). The identification module exploits 121

the temporal information between consecutive frames for identifying candidates of 122

potentially under- or over-seg cells, and feeds the information to CS-Net. CS-Net is 123

a context-aware, multi-scale machine learning architecture designed to automatically 124

reduce segmentation inaccuracies. It has been pretrained specifically for identifying 125

under-seg and over-seg. With the input from the identification module, CS-Net exam- 126

ines the candidate cells and their neighborhoods for segmentation refinement, then 127

returns to the identification module for reexamination.

128

After the identification module and CS-Net reach a self-consistent result of high 129

quality trajectories, LivecellX provides three downstream analysis modules. A biologi- 130

cal process detection module detects biological processes under interest such as mitosis 131

and apoptosis. Correct detection of mitosis enables reconstruction of cell lineages and 132

downstream analyses (Fig. 1h left). A cell feature extraction module optimized for 133

efficiency via parallelization quantifies high-dimensional features like morphology and 134

texture features and stores the information in single-cell objects (Fig. 1h middle). With 135

a third dynamics analysis module, one represents single cell states and trajectories 136

in a high-dimensional feature space (Fig. 1h right). The quantitative representation 137

enables users to uncover underlying patterns in cell behavior, identify dynamic cellu- 138

lar states, and cluster cells based on temporal representation to reveal processes such 139

as cell cycle progression, response to external stimuli or drug treatments, and disease- 140

related phenotypic changes, etc. All these downstream analyses are implemented based 141

on an object-oriented data framework (Fig. 1i). The framework significantly increases 142

the efficiency of information extraction and manipulation on trajectories.

143

To ensure robustness and usability, we developed an asynchronous graphical user 144

interface (GUI) based on Napari [42], allowing human-in-the-loop surveillance and 145

intervention at any stage of the analyses. The GUI includes a visualization window 146

that displays microscopy images alongside corresponding segmentation masks and 147

annotations (Fig. 1j). When users encounter errors in specific trajectories--such as 148

segmentation drift or mitotic mislabeling--the GUI enables both visual verification 149

and manual correction (Fig. 1j left and middle, Extended Data Fig. 1a), as well as 150

trajectory-level operations like deletion or refinement (Fig. 1j right,Extended Data 151

157 2.2 Correction segmentation errors with the CS-Net
158 Despite recent development of deep-learning-based segmentation methods, segmenta159 tion errors are generally unavoidable when processing large imaging datasets. Typical 160 segmentation errors include under-seg where two or more cells are incorrectly seg161 mented as one mask (Fig. 2a top), and over-seg where one cell is incorrectly segmented 162 into two or more masks (Fig. 2a middle) [43­45]. In addition, we defined a subtype 163 of over-seg error termed over-seg dropout error for cases where only a portion of the 164 single-cell is segmented while other portions are not (Fig. 2a bottom). 165 The candidate segmentation errors are identified using temporal context (see next 166 section) and cropped as region of interest (ROI) from the original images (Fig. 2b). 167 The trained CS-Net takes each ROI­ consisting of a candidate cell, its neighbor168 hood, and the segmentation mask ­as input and outputs a corrected mask as well as 169 assigns the segmentation status to one of four categories: under-seg, over-seg, over-seg 170 dropout, and no error (negative) (Fig. 2b). An ROI is labeled as negative if the cell is 171 correctly segmented and requires no correction. The CS-Net model comprises a fea172 ture encoder--based on either a vision transformer or a convolutional neural network 173 which compresses the ROI into a latent representation, followed by two subnetworks: 174 a segmentor and a classifier (Fig. 2c).
175 2.2.1 Training of CS-Net
176 CS-Net needs to be pretrained on curated training and testing datasets independent 177 of inputs from the identification module. A training dataset includes ROI samples 178 of all the four categories mentioned in the above section. To fully leverage anno179 tated data, we implemented synthetic data generation algorithms to introduce both 180 under-seg and over-seg samples, particularly to address class imbalance in our datasets 181 (Extended Data Fig. 2a-e). Having negative samples in the training dataset enhances 182 the CS-Net robustness from altering accurate segmentation results misidentified by 183 the identification module. 184 A key challenge for CS-Net is to target only erroneous cells within an ROI (Fig. 185 2d). CS-Net differs from typical segmentation models by concentrating on potentially 186 incorrect cell masks through adding an additional image channel, i.e., a focus mech187 anism. By focusing on individual cell cases, CS-Net allows for precise training and 188 development of specific performance metrics, enhancing control over cell subpopula189 tions. While the focus channel can be the mask or label mask, we found that the 190 Euclidean distance transform (EDT) of the mask (Fig. 2e)[46] work the most robustly 191 on guiding the deep learning model to concentrate during correction ( Section 5.2.2). 192 Furthermore, we implemented a normalization technique that scales the EDT values 193 to a fixed range of 1 to 5, producing a contour-map-style representation. This nor194 malized input helps CS-Net prioritize pixels that require correction (Fig. 2e right), 195 and mitigate numerical instability of varying EDT pixel values caused by different 196 cell sizes. The normalized EDT mask and the raw label-free channel image are then 197 jointly fed to CS-Net for correction. During postprocessing, the normalized EDT val198 ues further facilitate selecting an appropriate threshold for generating binary masks 199 (Section 5.2.2). 200 For under-seg, the focus channel highlights regions of individual cells that need 201 separation, whereas over-seg requires identifying multiple fragments of a single cell 202 for merging, which typically relies on manual annotation or auxiliary detection 203 algorithms. To avoid these complexities, we augmented the training data with over204 seg dropout samples, each containing a single fragment from an over-seg case (see 205 Section 5.2), enabling CS-Net to reconstruct full cell masks from partial, fragmented

2.2.2 Evaluating CS-Net

210

Fig. 2f shows representative corrections of under-seg, over-seg, and over-seg dropout. 211

These inputs span varying scales and background padding, all of which CS-Net 212

robustly handled. The model generalizes across different types of microscopy images 213

with high confluence of cells (Extended Data Fig. 3a). Additionally, CS-Net resolves 214

non-trivial errors beyond canonical under- and over-seg like adjacent-cell merges, 215

boundary ambiguities, three-cell under-seg, and uncategorized mis-segmentation 216

(Extended Data Fig. 3b).

217

We evaluated segmentation performance using four metrics: cell match rate, 218

correction success rate, intersection over union (IoU) calculated on cell contours (mor- 219

phology), and root mean square error (RMSE) of EDT masks (Extended Data Fig. 220

3c-e; see Methods for definitions). Our evaluation employs instance-level mask-based 221

metrics that are more sensitive to mask errors.

222

Statistical analysis of mask-based metrics showed that CS-Net with the focus 223

mechanism significantly improved segmentation quality (Fig. 2g). CS-Net success- 224

fully corrected nearly all over-seg error cases. In under-seg scenarios, CS-Net achieved 225

strong performance across three of the four evaluation metrics, with the exception 226

of the conventional binary mask IoU, which is commonly used in the field (Fig. 2g). 227

This metric is not well suited for evaluating under-seg, as erroneous and ground-truth 228

masks often appear nearly identical at the pixel level despite representing different 229

cell instances(Fig. 2f). Consequently, even a perfect correction by CS-Net may result 230

in minimal or no change in the binary mask IoU. In contrast, RMSE of EDT masks, 231

cell match rate, and correction success rate provide more sensitive and informative 232

assessments for under-seg cases by directly evaluating instance correspondence and 233

morphological accuracy.

234

We compared the performance of CS-Net with and without the focus mechanism 235

under different conditions (Fig. 2h­j). For under-seg and negative cases, the perfor- 236

mance gap between the two models increases as padding size grows, reflecting the 237

presence of more cells in the label-free image crop. For over-seg cases, the gap widens 238

as the IoU threshold becomes more stringent, demonstrating the advantage of the 239

focus mechanism under stricter criteria. When no padding is applied, however, over- 240

seg crops often contain only a single cell, in which case the focus mechanism provides 241

little benefit. Overall, CS-Net with the focus mechanism achieved consistently higher 242

cell match percentages across all conditions, maintaining robust performance across 243

varying background sizes, as controlled by different padding values from raw imag- 244

ing data (Extend Data Fig. 3 f-h). Notably, the performance gap favoring the focus 245

mechanism expanded with larger padding sizes, underscoring its effectiveness in han- 246

dling diverse spatial contexts. The comparison of correction success rates at different 247

padding size, IoU thresholds, microscopy modalities and error types on in different 248

data subsets also reveals the robustness of this method(Extend Data Fig. 3 i-j).

249

The accuracy of the classifier was also higher when CS-Net is equipped with the 250

focus mechanism. This aligns with intuition: without the focus mechanism, CS-Net 251

lacks information about which specific cell within the ROI should be classified and 252

segmented, leading to reduced performances (Fig. 2k).

263 2.3 Linkage correction in single cell trajectories with CS-Net
264 Cells are deformable, often exist in high-density environments, and can divide, dis265 appear, or occlude each other. While existing tracking algorithms have been adapted 266 to account for these biological constraints, they generally assume that the input 267 segmentation masks are accurate [47­49]. 268 However, segmentation errors cause several typical linkage distortions between 269 single-cell masks including cascade of wrong linkages involving neighboring cells caused 270 by under-seg of two cells from different trajectories(Fig. 3a), decreased trajectory qual271 ity and erroneous linkages caused by unassigned over-seg fragments (Fig. 3b), missing 272 frames due to absent cells (Fig. 3c, Extended Data Fig. 4a, details in Methods). 273 Linkage errors can be further complicated when more neighboring cells are involved. 274 Robust lineage reconstruction requires jointly correcting segmentation masks and 275 tracking linkages, motivating integrated approaches that iteratively refine both. In 276 CS-Net, candidate errors are first detected and cropped as ROIs. Note that there are 277 typically strong overlap between correct masks across consecutive frames, and seg278 mentation errors appear as abrupt changes in overlap or morphology (Fig. 3a-c). To 279 capture these, an inconsistency detection module (TD-1) filters temporal inconsisten280 cies using IoU and Intersection over Minimum (IoMin) metrics (Extended Data Fig. 281 4b). Each under-seg candidate was represented as a triplet {celltu, cell1t+t, cell2t+t}, 282 where celltu denotes the under-seg mask. Each over-seg candidate was represented as 283 a pair {cellot , cellt+t}, where cellot denotes the over-seg mask (Extended Data Fig. 284 4c). Candidates meeting the error criteria are cropped with surrounding regions and 285 passed to CS-Net for correction (Fig. 3d). The candidate triplets are affected by the 286 threshold values of IoU and IoMin scores (Extended Data Fig. 4d-e). In addition, the 287 efficiency of detection is affected by situations like mitosis and cells with high motility. 288 Testing on multiple datasets revealed that the TD-1 method alone was insufficient 289 for detecting segmentation errors, particularly when errors occurred in consecutive 290 frames along a single-cell trajectory (Extended Data Fig. 4f). To address this limita291 tion, we developed a multi-iteration, trajectory-level algorithm that employs CS-Net 292 to iteratively correct both segmentation and track linkage errors (Fig. 3e). In each 293 iteration, trajectory-level errors are identified, corrected by CS-Net, and the corre294 sponding trajectories updated. This process ensures that the first error in a sequence 295 of persistent errors is resolved in the first iteration, while subsequent errors are cor296 rected in later iterations. Typical linkage errors--caused by under-seg (Fig. 3f, left), 297 over-seg (Fig. 3f, middle), and absent cells (Fig. 3f, right)--are successfully corrected 298 by this approach. 299 Validation on A549 trajectories showed that, after fifty iterations, the number 300 of attempted and resolved under-seg corrections reached saturation (Fig. 3g). As 301 expected, a clear gap remained between potential errors flagged by TD-1 and true 302 errors corrected by CS-Net, reflecting that CS-Net can detect false positives included 303 in TD-1 detection (Extended Data Fig. 4g-h). The ROC analysis (Fig. 3h) further 304 demonstrated that CS-Net with trajectory inconsistency detection achieved an AUC 305 of 97%, outperforming the 87% AUC from TD-1 features alone. Visual inspection 306 confirmed that all true over-seg errors were correctly identified and corrected. 307 Next, we compared trajectory lengths before and after our correction procedure 308 (Fig. 3i). For each corrected trajectory, we calculated the relative increase in length. 309 Across all tested settings of the maximum age parameter(max gap)in the tracking 310 algorithm (Methods), the median relative increase exceeded 25%, demonstrating the 311 robustness of our correction method (Fig. 3i). Notably, when max gap was small, the 312 relative increase approached 40%, as shorter max gap values tend to produce more 313 fragmented trajectories. In line with this, the total number of trajectories decreased 314 substantially after correction, reflecting the successful linking of short trajectory 315 fragments (Fig. 3j).

2.4 A biological process detection module allows user-specified 321

cell biology event-aware image analyses

322

Tracking cells in live-cell images is uniquely challenging due to biological processes 323

such as division and apoptosis. Cell division, in particular, adds complexity to single- 324

cell dynamics and lineage analysis. When a mother cell divides, its trajectory should 325

terminate while two daughter trajectories are initiated, with the mother­daughter 326

relationship preserved for lineage reconstruction. Without accurate mitosis detection, 327

tracking algorithms may incorrectly link trajectories or miss lineage relationships (Fig. 328

4a).

329

Therefore, we developed a module to identify frames where user-specified biological 330

processes occur. As an example, we trained a network to detect mitosis, which is 331

marked by rapid morphological changes; in 2D cultures, both mother and daughter 332

cells appear small and round (Fig. 4b).

333

We integrated video-based deep learning methods by leveraging full temporal and 334

visual context. Two frameworks -- CNN- and transformer-based (Fig. 4c) -- were fine- 335

tuned from MMAction video-classification models [50] and adapted to treat mitosis 336

detection as a single-cell trajectory classification task. Detected events are stored in 337

the LivecellX trajectory structure, though manual annotation may still be required 338

for data from different microscopes or conditions. Alternatively, users can identify 339

divisions via the GUI.

340

Fig. 4d shows an A549 single-cell lineage spanning three generations, revealing 341

differences in motility and cell cycle duration among sister and cousin cells. LivecellX 342

also enables statistical analysis of cell cycle durations (Fig. 4e) and visualization of 343

multiple reconstructed lineages across generations, including spatial positions (Fig. 4f, 344

left) and displacements relative to the initial frame (Fig. 4f, right).

2.5 Feature extraction and dynamics analysis modules provide 346

tools for cell dynamics studies

347

A key downstream task in LivecellX is extracting multiplex features from fluores- 348

cent and/or label-free images to represent single-cell trajectories in multidimensional 349

feature space. These features enable visualization, interpretation, and modeling of 350

cellular dynamics. LivecellX integrates tools such as scikit-image and mahotas to 351

extract morphological and texture-based descriptors (e.g., Haralick features), which 352

strongly correlate with cell states and capture dynamic changes over time. Impor- 353

tantly, many morphological features can be quantified from transmitted-light images, 354

avoiding the limitations of fluorescence labeling, including restricted marker capacity 355

and phototoxicity.

356

As a demonstration, we extracted multidimensional morphological and texture 357

features from time-lapse images of two cell lines, MCF10A and A549. Feature distri- 358

butions differed between the lines (Fig. 5a), and cell line­specific clusters emerged in 359

t-SNE and UMAP embeddings (Fig. 5b). These embeddings correlated with specific 360

morphological and texture features(Fig. 5c, Extend Data Fig. 5a), whose contribu- 361

tions were further examined by principal component analysis (PCA) (Extended Data 362

Fig. 5b).

363

LivecellX enhances the quality of extracted trajectories in long-term live cell imag- 364

ing and enables exploration of dynamic changes along each trajectory. The original 365

images at different time points of a typical single cell trajectory can be retrieved con- 366

veniently with data framework of LivecellX (Fig. 5d left). The morphology features 367

including cell area, major axis length(MAL) and eccentricity are shown as time series 368

3 Discussion

426

In this study, we present LivecellX, a comprehensive pipeline for analyzing single- 427

cell dynamics from time-lapse imaging data. Built on a single-cell object-oriented 428

framework, LivecellX integrates segmentation, tracking, feature extraction, and trajec- 429

tory analysis while minimizing technical overhead and coding complexity. To address 430

common challenges such as inaccurate segmentation, tracking errors, and lineage 431

reconstruction, it combines automated correction with two fallback options: invalidat- 432

ing those single cell objects or trajectories containing errors, or manual intervention 433

with GUI to manually correct segmentation errors or other issues that algorithms were 434

unable to resolve. LivecellX provides a new method for resolving the segmentation and 435

tracking errors at the same time, while the segmentation and tracking outputs can 436

be generated with classical methods. One alternative way is performing segmentation 437

and tracking simultaneously [25].

438

Leveraging machine learning, LivecellX reduces labeling burden by pretraining 439

segmentation on public cell image datasets and employing semi-automatic algorithms 440

for labeling incorrect segmentation and key events such as mitosis and apoptosis. 441

LivecellX integrates transmitted-light and fluorescence features and leverages deep 442

learning advances to extract additional information from label-free images such as 443

organelle identification [55, 56]. In future iterations of LivecellX, we aim to incorporate 444

more features to further enhance its functionality. Therefore, LivecellX is designed as 445

an open, extensible framework. It supports future advances in segmentation, tracking, 446

and integration with other analysis pipelines, enabling more robust and comprehensive 447

studies of cellular dynamics.

480 5.2 Corrective Segmentation Network (CS-Net)
481 CS-Net is designed to refine erroneous segmentation masks by operating on cropped 482 ROIs where single-cell segmentation errors are detected. It is implemented as a mod483 ular architecture using PyTorch Lightning and supports interchangeable backbones.
16

In its default configuration, it employs a DeepLabV3-ResNet50 backbone pretrained 484

on ImageNet, with the final classifier replaced by a 1 × 1 convolutional layer to 485

produce multi-class output. An auxiliary classifier is also attached to intermedi- 486

ate backbone features to predict categorical segmentation error types (e.g., over- or 487

under-segmentation).

488

Input

489

For each identified error region, an ROI including the erroneous mask and its surround- 490

ing context is cropped from the original image and mask. Input tensors are shaped as 491

3 × H × W , where H and W denote the height and width of the cropped ROI. The 492

first channel contains the raw image. The second channel is a derived feature map 493

(e.g., binary mask, label mask, Euclidean distance transform (EDT), or normalized 494

EDT) as described in Section 5.2.2. The third channel duplicates the second to meet 495

the three-channel requirement of pretrained backbones.

496

Output

497

CS-Net produces three output masks:

498

· Segmentation mask ­ A probability map representing the likelihood of each pixel 499

belonging to a true cell object.

500

· Under-seg mask ­ A probability map indicating the likelihood of pixels lying 501

outside the true object boundary but erroneously included in the original cell mask. 502

· Over-seg mask ­ A probability map indicating the likelihood of pixels that should 503

be included in the cell mask but were mistakenly excluded.

504

Loss functions

505

The network supports cross-entropy (CE), mean squared error (MSE), and binary 506

cross-entropy (BCE) loss functions, selected based on input types. While class and 507

pixel-wise weighting can be applied during training, we found little performance 508

difference with or without them. For model training, we use BCE as the loss func- 509

tion, specifically tailored to our requirements. Because each pixel may belong to 510

multiple channels, it is inappropriate to use the standard cross-entropy across all 511

classes. Instead, we compute the MSE alongside BCE, incorporating EDT to facilitate 512

comparative evaluation.

513

During ablation tests, we explored various strategies for augmenting the other two 514

channels. One approach involved removing backgrounds from raw images to create 515

background-free representations of single cells in one of both channels. Additionally, 516

we examined the effectiveness of incorporating segmentation masks and single-cell 517

masks as two additional channels. This methodology enables us to assess the impact 518

of these extra channels on the model's performance.

5.3 Trajectory correction algorithms

683

In live-cell imaging, there are three major typical linkage distortions between single-cell 684

masks caused by segmentation errors:

685

1. Under-seg of two cells from different trajectories: When two cells, one from 686

trajectory A and the other from trajectory B, are under-segmented at time t, they 687

are merged into a single mask and mistakenly assigned to the same trajectory. If the 688

merged object is assigned to trajectory A, trajectory B typically loses a time point. 689

In some cases, another nearby cell at time t may be incorrectly assigned to trajectory 690

B instead (Fig. 3a). This type of wrong linkage can propagate, leading to a cascade 691

of incorrect linkages during tracking, particularly those involving neighboring cells. 692

2. Over-seg of a single cell: If a cell at time t in a trajectory is segmented into 693

multiple fragments, only one fragment is associated with the trajectory. The situation 694

constitutes a typical over-seg case. Unlike under-seg, over-seg errors do not often result 695

in direct tracking linkage failures. However, they can still degrade the quality of down- 696

stream trajectory analysis. In rare instances where the segmentation model frequently 697

produces over-seg errors, the unassigned fragments may interfere with the tracking of 698

neighboring cells, potentially leading to incorrect trajectory linkages (Fig. 3b).

699

3. Absent cell: When the segmentation algorithm fails to detect an existing cell 700

at time t, no mask is produced for that location. As a result, the tracking algorithm 701

may terminate the trajectory prematurely or mis-assign the cell in subsequent frames. 702

This type of error can also cause confusion in nearby trajectories if the algorithm 703

incorrectly links neighboring cells to compensate for the missing one (Fig. 3c).

704

To efficiently address tracking and segmentation errors in large-scale live-cell 705

imaging, we developed an iterative trajectory correction algorithm that integrates 706

spatial-temporal consistency checks with CS-Net (Fig. 3e). The method is parameter- 707

ized by values including a temporal search window (3 frames), correction crop padding 708

(20 pixels), a maximum number of correction iterations (default 100), and an exclusion 709

zone for boundary cells (50 pixels). All parameters are tunable.

710

For under-seg, the algorithm inspects each trajectory for missing frames. When a 711

gap is found, it examines whether any cell masks present in the missing frame overlap 712

substantially with the trajectory's masks in adjacent frames, using an IoMin/IoU 713

threshold=0.5. If such matches exist, the candidate error region is cropped and passed 714

to the trained CS-Net, which classifies and corrects the segmentation. The resulting 715

corrected masks are assigned back to the trajectories using the linear assignment 716

problem (LAP) based on spatial relation. If the correction is successful, the trajectories 717

are updated accordingly: splitting, merging, or extending as needed.

718

For over-seg, the criterion we used in the reported analyses is based on morpho- 719

logical dynamics. Here, the algorithm monitors the area of each segmented cell mask 720

Harmony VAE for Microscopy Image Disentanglement

788

We adopted the original Harmony framework [51], a generic VAE-based model 789

designed to disentangle semantic content from parameterized transformations in an 790

unsupervised manner. Harmony combines a standard encoder­decoder architecture 791

with a cross-contrastive learning objective to separate transformation-specific and 792

content-specific latent factors. In its original form, Harmony demonstrated strong per- 793

formance on structured datasets such as MNIST and cryo-EM images. However, we 794

found that this architecture does not generalize effectively for our label-free microscopy 795

datasets, where high noise levels and fine-scale cellular heterogeneity pose additional 796

challenges. A primary limitation of the original model in our setting was posterior 797

collapse, where the learned latent variables failed to capture meaningful variation 798

and reconstructions became nearly identical regardless of latent input [8]. Addition- 799

ally, the model often produced nearly identical reconstructions across different latent 800

codes, indicating poor disentanglement and underfitting of the semantic space. To 801

address these shortcomings, we replaced Harmony's original fully connected encoder 802

and decoder with convolutional architectures tailored for high-resolution microscopy 803

images. Specifically, we employed a deeper convolutional encoder with residual blocks 804

and batch normalization to extract hierarchical visual features, and a mirrored trans- 805

posed convolutional decoder to enhance spatial resolution during reconstruction. These 806

architectural changes substantially increased the model capacity and improved its 807

ability to capture both global cell shape and fine-grained morphological variations. 808

We retained Harmony's cross-contrastive loss structure, which includes three dissim- 809

ilarity terms--between the image reconstructed from semantic latent space and the 810

image transformed by the predicted transformation parameters, between the trans- 811

formed outputs of original and augmented views, and between the reconstruction from 812

the augmented input and its transformed version--along with a KL divergence term 813

enforcing proximity of semantic latent distributions between similar inputs. These 814

components jointly drive the disentanglement of latent representations while avoid- 815

ing trivial solutions. In our implementation, we further regularized transformation 816

parameters through appropriate activation functions and shared kernel weights. We 817

empirically found that our convolutional Harmony variant better disentangles latent 818

content from cell shape variability and imaging transformations. This enhanced for- 819

mulation enables generation of synthetic microscopy images and improves robustness 820

to transformation-induced noise.
